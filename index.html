The Story

What is Holi?

Holi is one of the most beloved festivals in the Hindu calendar — a riotous celebration of color, joy, and the victory of good over evil. Celebrated on the full moon day of the month of Phalgun, it marks the arrival of spring.

The festival is rooted in the legend of Prahlada and Holika, and also celebrates the divine love of Radha and Krishna. It is a day when social barriers dissolve, and people drench each other in vivid gulal powders and water.

From the streets of Mathura to cities around the world, Holi transforms ordinary spaces into living, breathing canvases of pure joy.

The Celebration

How Holi Unfolds

Evening Before

Holika Dahan 🔥

The night before Holi, bonfires are lit across the country to commemorate the burning of the demoness Holika and the triumph of devotion over evil. People gather to perform rituals, sing folk songs, and ward off negativity.

Morning

Rangwali Holi — The Color Play 🎨

As dawn breaks, streets explode with color. Children and adults alike throw gulal powder and spray colored water with pichkaris (water guns). Everyone is fair game — no one stays unstained.

Midday

Music & Dance 🥁

Dhol drums beat through neighborhoods. Crowds dance in the streets. Classical and folk songs celebrating Krishna and Radha fill the air. The joy is collective, contagious, and wholly uninhibited.

Afternoon

Thandai & Sweets 🍬

After the revelry, families share thandai (a spiced milk drink), gujiya (sweet dumplings), puran poli, and papri chaat. Food and feasting bind neighbors and strangers alike into one joyful family.

Evening

Milni — Forgiveness & Reunion 🙏

As the day winds down, people visit relatives and friends, embrace, and exchange sweets. Old grudges are forgiven. New relationships are formed. Holi is a reset for the soul.
